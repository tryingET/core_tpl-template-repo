# CONSTRAINT INVENTORY — Real vs Imagined

## Invoke When
- Feeling limited
- "We can't do that because..."
- Seeking freedom within constraints

## The Framework

List every constraint on the solution.

For each, ask:
- Who said so?
- When did they say so?
- What would happen if we violated it?
- Is it a law of physics or a law of convention?

Categories:
- HARD CONSTRAINTS — Cannot be violated (physics, math, regulations)
- SOFT CONSTRAINTS — Violable but expensive (time, money, politics)
- FALSE CONSTRAINTS — Violable and cheap (we just think we can't)

Rank false constraints by the freedom gained by discarding them.

The prize: Every false constraint removed expands the solution space dramatically.

## Output Template

```markdown
## CONSTRAINT INVENTORY

### Constraint List

| Constraint | Source | Category | Violation Consequence |
|------------|--------|----------|----------------------|
| [constraint] | [who/when] | HARD / SOFT / FALSE | [what happens] |

### False Constraints (Freedom Available)
1. [Constraint] → Freedom gained: [what becomes possible]
2. ...

### Soft Constraints (Freedom Available at Cost)
1. [Constraint] → Cost of violation: [price]
2. ...

### Hard Constraints (Accept and Work Within)
1. [Constraint] → Why immutable: [reason]
2. ...

### Expanded Solution Space
[What's now possible that wasn't before]
```

## Why It Works

Most constraints are self-imposed. This surfaces them and forces explicit categorization, often revealing that the "impossible" is merely the "uncomfortable."
